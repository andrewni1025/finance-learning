🧭 Before You Invest

Before learning how to buy, decide whether you should buy, how much to invest, and what actually fits your situation.

Do These 4 Things First

1

Build an emergency fund

Keep 3-6 months of living expenses in cash before investing. Without a safety buffer, a market drop can force you to sell at the worst time.

2

Pay off high-interest debt

Credit card installments, consumer loans, and online loans often cost more than what most beginners can reasonably earn from investing.

3

Get basic protection in place

Medical, accident, and critical illness coverage matter. A single emergency can wipe out your portfolio if you are not protected.

4

Set your time horizon

Money you need within 1 year should not be in high-volatility assets. Money you can leave for 5-10 years is much better suited for equities.

ETF Selection Checklist

  • Expense ratio: lower costs matter when you hold for years.
  • Tracking error: check whether the ETF actually follows its benchmark closely.
  • Size and liquidity: larger, more liquid ETFs often have tighter spreads.
  • Holdings structure: look at top holdings, country exposure, and industry concentration.
  • Distribution method: income-distributing vs accumulating matters for your goal.
  • Index itself: know whether it tracks the S&P 500, Nasdaq 100, CSI 300, or a sector theme.

Two Numbers You Should Not Ignore

1. Total trading cost

  • Broker commission and platform fees
  • Bid-ask spread
  • FX conversion costs
  • Stamp duty and exchange levies
  • Margin interest and stock borrow fees

2. Maximum drawdown

Return is not the only metric. If an asset falls 50%, it needs to rise 100% just to recover. Ask yourself: how much drawdown can I tolerate without panic-selling?

Example: if ¥100,000 falls to ¥50,000, you lost 50%. To return from ¥50,000 to ¥100,000, you need a 100% gain.

📚 Chapter 1: Stock Market Basics

Before you put real money at risk, understand these core ideas first.

What is a stock?

A stock is a piece of ownership in a company. When you buy shares, you become a shareholder. If the company earns money, you may receive dividends; if the business becomes more valuable, your shares may appreciate.

Example: Apple (AAPL) has more than 15 billion shares outstanding. If you own 100 shares, you own a tiny slice of the company.

How is the stock price determined?

Price is driven by supply and demand.

  • More buyers than sellers → price rises
  • More sellers than buyers → price falls

Company earnings, industry trends, macro data, sentiment, and policy changes all affect that demand.

Bull vs Bear Market

🐂 Bull Market

A sustained rising market with strong confidence. A common rule of thumb is a gain of 20% or more.

🐻 Bear Market

A sustained falling market with weak sentiment. A common rule of thumb is a fall of 20% or more from a recent high.

Read this before your first trade

  • Buying a stock is not the same as buying a business you fully understand.
  • A good company can still be a bad buy if the price is too high.
  • If you cannot explain why you own something, you probably should not own it.
  • Do not confuse recent price movement with long-term value.
